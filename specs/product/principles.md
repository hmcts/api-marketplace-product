# Product Principles

The API Marketplace provides a trusted environment where APIs are published, discovered, governed, and consumed as products—enabling safe reuse, consistent integration, and faster delivery across organisational and partner boundaries.

## 1. Foster API-first integration

The API Marketplace promotes an API‑first approach to integration. APIs should be treated as the primary interface from the outset of service design rather than being introduced later as an afterthought.

This ensures that capabilities are accessible, composable, and interoperable through consistent, well‑designed interfaces. The Marketplace maintains a unified API catalogue that enables consumers to easily discover, understand, and reuse available APIs.

## 2. Make the right thing the easy thing

The Marketplace is designed to be simple, fast, and intuitive to use. Pre‑approved patterns, standards, and tooling ensure that compliant approaches are the path of least resistance. This reduces friction for teams and removes the need for bespoke or non‑standard integration approaches.

## 3. Focus on the boundary

The Marketplace prioritises APIs that operate at organisational, partner, and cross‑jurisdiction boundaries, where clear, well‑governed interfaces are essential. Internal service‑to‑service APIs may also be published in the catalogue to support discovery and reuse across HMCTS.

By concentrating on these boundaries, the Marketplace maximises business value, applies appropriate levels of security and governance, and provides a clear and consistent interface for external consumers and partners.

## 4. Standards-based approach

Consistent standards are essential for adoption, usability, and long‑term maintainability.

All APIs published through the Marketplace adhere to clear, well-defined standards, including:

* Contract-first design – API contracts are defined, reviewed, and agreed upon before implementation.
* Lifecycle management – Every API has a named owner and a transparent lifecycle from publication through versioning to deprecation. 
* Documentation standards – APIs are documented clearly and consistently to support rapid onboarding. 
* Versioning and compatibility – Changes are managed in a predictable way to minimise disruption to consumers.

## 5. Enable product teams to own and scale independently

The API Marketplace enables API adoption through standards, tooling, guardrails, and self‑service capabilities.

The Marketplace does not build, own, or operate the APIs listed in the catalogue. Product teams retain full end‑to‑end ownership of their APIs—from design through operation and support.

This federated model avoids creating a central integration bottleneck and enables teams to move faster while scaling independently.

## 6. Enable reuse

A core objective of the Marketplace is to maximise reuse of existing APIs.

Reuse is encouraged through an intuitive catalogue, clear documentation, streamlined onboarding processes, and a strong focus on developer experience and partner support.

## 7. Incremental improvements

The Marketplace evolves through frequent, incremental improvements informed by real‑world usage and partner feedback.

This approach avoids large up‑front design assumptions, reduces delivery risk, and ensures the platform continues to meet the needs of its users over time.

## 8. Embedded governance

Governance is embedded directly into Marketplace standards, automation, and self‑service tooling rather than relying on manual review processes or central approval gates.

This approach ensures compliance while avoiding unnecessary overhead, delays, and governance‑related bottlenecks.

## 9. In-built security

Security, assurance, and compliance are built directly into Marketplace tooling and processes.

This provides a consistent, best‑practice approach and removes the need for providers and consumers to implement bespoke security solutions. Security is treated as an enabler of safe adoption rather than a barrier to delivery.

## 10. In-built observability

APIs published through the Marketplace should be observable end‑to‑end.

Usage patterns, performance metrics, availability, and other operational insights are made visible in a transparent and accessible way, enabling providers and consumers to make informed operational and product decisions.
